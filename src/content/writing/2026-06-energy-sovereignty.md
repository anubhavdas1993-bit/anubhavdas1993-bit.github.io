There is a version of the sustainable-fuel story told entirely in penalties. A mandate here, a fine there, and the assumption follows that the fear of those numbers is what moves a buyer to commit. I left the investor's seat to work first-of-a-kind synthetic fuel directly, and I watched that assumption fail in the room.

## The lever everyone reaches for

We had a regulation-backed alternative and every reason to believe the compliance stick would do the work. It did not. The penalty set the stage; it never closed the deal.

> The obvious lever, the fear of high fines, was the thing that moved them least.

That is not a comfortable thing to learn when your whole model assumes it. But it points at something more durable than any subsidy.

## What actually closed it

What closed the deal was the upside, and the upside was sovereignty. Security of supply. Energy independence. Not depending on a volatile resource that someone else controls, and underneath that, insulation from a spot market that swings, with pricing that rewarded whoever committed first.

The thing we thought we were selling and the thing they were actually buying were different things, and you only learn that in the room. The deadlock did not break on cost or on compliance. It broke on structure, on the carrots assembled in the right order.

## The lesson generalises

Scaling hard technology is never one thing. It is a commercial reality built piece by piece around a molecule that, by the time the deal is real, is the least interesting part of it. The subsidy is a stage prop. The supply story is the plot.
